Solar System Facts

Mercury

Thirteen times a century Mercury can be observed from the Earth passing across the face of the Sun in an event called a transit, the next will occur on the 9th May 2016.

Venus

Venus is the second brightest object in the night sky after the Moon. The surface of the planet is obscured by an opaque layer of clouds made up of sulfuric acid.

Earth

Unlike the other planets that are named after classic deities the Earth’s name comes from the Anglo-Saxon word erda which means ground or soil. The Earth was formed approximately 4.54 billion years ago and is the only known planet to support life.

Mars

Often described as the “Red Planet” due to its reddish appearance. Mars is a terrestrial planet with a thin atmosphere composed primarily of carbon dioxide.

Flip the cards for more Solar System facts

  • How long is Mercury's year?

    It takes Mercury just under 88 (Earth) days to orbit the sun.
    It's orbit is the least like a circle of all the planets.

  • What's weird about Venus?

    It rotates counter-clockwise, unlike the other planets in the Solar System.
    Venus has no moons nor rings.

  • Where is Earth's water from?

    Earth's water was initially trapped within the planet; however, over time this water was brought to surface as a result of the planet's volcanic activity.

  • Earth and Mars both have..?

    They are the only 2 planets with polar ice caps!

  • How many moons does Jupiter have?

    Jupiter has at least 67 moons.

  • What's an unusual quality of Saturn?

    Saturn is the least dense planet in the Solar System. It has a density less than water, so it would float!

  • What's odd about Uranus's axis?

    The axis of Uranus is 98 degrees, so it almost lies sideways as it orbits the sun. Its north and south poles on lie near where the equator is on Earth!

  • Neptune's orbit is how long?

    165 (Earth) years. On 11 July 2011, Neptune made one complete orbit of the sun from the day it was discovered
